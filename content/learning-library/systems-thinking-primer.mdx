## ðŸ” What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking helps you see the **whole picture** â€” the connections, patterns, and root causes behind problems, not just the visible symptoms.

Instead of asking "How do we fix this broken thing?" systems thinking asks:
- **Why is it broken?** (Root causes)
- **Who and what is connected to it?** (Actors and relationships)
- **Where can small changes create big impact?** (Leverage points)

## ðŸ§© Four Key Elements to Map

When analyzing any problem, look for these four elements:

### 1. **Actors** 
Who are the people, organizations, or entities involved?

*Example (Market Waste):* Vendors, customers, waste collectors, local government, recycling cooperatives, drainage maintenance workers

### 2. **Flows**
What moves through the system? (Materials, money, information, trust)

*Example:* 
- Waste flows from vendors â†’ drains â†’ flooding
- Money flows from customers â†’ vendors
- Information flows through word-of-mouth networks

### 3. **Constraints**
What limits or blocks desired outcomes?

*Example:*
- Collection costs too high for individual vendors
- No trust in payment systems
- Lack of nearby recycling facilities
- Cultural norms around waste disposal

### 4. **Leverage Points**
Where can small interventions create disproportionate positive change?

*Example:*
- A visible collection drum at market entrance (changes behavior)
- Micro-payments via mobile money (removes cost barrier)
- One enthusiastic vendor champion (builds trust)

## ðŸŽ¨ Practical Exercise: Map Your System

Follow these steps to create a simple system map for your problem:

### Step 1: Draw the Actors (5 minutes)
- Write down all the people/groups involved
- Circle the ones with the most power or influence
- Star the ones most affected by the problem

### Step 2: Map the Flows (5 minutes)
- Draw arrows showing what moves between actors
- Label each arrow (waste, money, information, etc.)
- Use different colors for different types of flows

### Step 3: Identify Constraints (5 minutes)
- Write down what's blocking desired outcomes
- Categorize: Physical? Economic? Social? Technical?
- Mark which constraints you might be able to influence

### Step 4: Find Leverage Points (10 minutes)
- Look for places where multiple flows intersect
- Identify actors who could become champions
- Find small changes that could shift behavior patterns

## ðŸ’¡ Key Questions for Your Team

Use these prompts to deepen your systems understanding:

### Understanding Interconnections
- What happens upstream and downstream of our intervention?
- Who benefits if this problem continues? (Sometimes problems persist because they serve someone)
- What unintended consequences might our solution create?

### Finding Root Causes
- Ask "Why?" five times to get past symptoms
- What would happen if we did nothing?
- What's the difference between what people say and what they do?

### Designing Interventions
- Where are the feedback loops? (What reinforces current behavior?)
- Can we work with existing flows instead of creating new ones?
- What's the smallest intervention that could test our assumptions?
